Module 03 - More Notes on Probability

"I dreamed of the number 7 for seven straight nights," he said, "and 7 times 7 is 48." Those of us with a better command of our multiplication tables might chuckle at the man’s error, but we all create our own view of the world and then employ it to filter and process our perceptions, extracting meaning from the ocean of data that washes over us in daily life. And we often make errors that, though less obvious, are just as significant as his.

I. Introduction & Foundations

  • A logical misinterpretation of simple math or random wins highlights how flawed human intuition can be regarding chance and cognitive bias. This establishes why we build statistical models to evaluate uncertainty.
  • Data Does Not Speak for Itself: Numbers contain no inherent meaning alone; they must be contextually parsed, scrubbed, and interpreted within analytical frameworks.
  • Deterministic vs. Probabilistic: Deterministic workflows produce predictable outputs from rigid inputs, whereas probabilistic architectures are built to measure optimal routes under uncertainty.
  • Risk vs. Uncertainty: In finance and forecasting, Risk contains an unknown outcome but a thoroughly verified, known probability distribution. True Uncertainty exists when both the final outcome and its underlying probability distribution are entirely unmapped.
  • Variance: Measures the dispersion of a random variable, quantifying how far observed data expands away from the population mean.

II. Operational Axioms & Combinatorics

  • Probability Scales: Measured precisely from 0 (impossible event) to 1 (absolute certainty). Calculated as the number of targeted favorable outcomes divided by the total count of the uniform sample space.
  • Counting Rules: Combinations, permutations, and factorials serve as structural backbones to calculate total sample spaces across varied sampling criteria.
  • Set Operations: Intersections (AND), unions (OR), complements (NOT), and conditional constraints form the algebraic foundational layer of probability calculations.
The Addition Rule

Used to calculate the probability of either one event or another occurring (Union).

  • Mutually Exclusive Events: Events cannot happen simultaneously. P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B).
  • Non-Mutually Exclusive Events: Events overlap. P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B).

Example: Probability of rolling a 1 or a 6 on a six-sided die. Since they are mutually exclusive, 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3.

The Multiplication Rule

Used to calculate the joint probability of two events occurring together (Intersection).

  • Independent Events: One event does not impact the other. P(A and B) = P(A) * P(B).
  • Dependent Events: One event alters the sample space of the next. P(A and B) = P(A) * P(B|A).

Example: Drawing two Aces consecutively from a 52-card deck without replacement. Dependent multiplication dictates: (4/52) * (3/51) = 12/2652 = 1/221.

III. Statistical Fallacies & Decision Cases

The Conjunction Fallacy

A cognitive bias where individuals judge that a specific conjunction of two combined events is statistically more likely than a single, broader baseline component on its own, violating basic intersection limits.

The Prosecutor's Fallacy

A common mistake in legal reasoning and conditional reasoning that completely swaps conditional statements. It confuses the probability of an evidence profile being present given a suspect is innocent, P(Evidence | Innocence), with the actual probability of innocence given that evidence profile is present, P(Innocence | Evidence).

Case Study Example: A rare blood match matching a defendant is found at a crime scene, present in only 1% of the population. A prosecutor mistakenly claims there is a 99% chance of guilt based on that alone. This is flawed because it completely ignores the prior probability of the defendant being guilty before the blood profile was assessed.

O.J. Simpson: Conditional Probabilities in Litigation

A classic demonstration of opposing conditional claims in historical legal defense analysis:

  • The defense argued that the baseline probability of a domestic abuser going on to kill their spouse is extremely low (most abusers do not kill).
  • The prosecution countered with the relevant flipped conditional probability: among spouses who are abused and end up murdered, the vast majority are killed by their abusing partner.

The Monty Hall Problem

A classic probability teaser demonstrating how conditional choices shift sample spaces, proving that counter-intuitive switching always yields a 2/3 probability of winning compared to maintaining the initial choice (1/3).

IV. Inference: Frequentists vs. Bayesians

Modern data pipelines split into two structural design perspectives depending on how parameters and empirical samples are treated:

Approach Probability Interpretation Use of Prior Information Statistical Inference
Frequentist Long-run frequency of repeatable events Strictly avoided; relies purely on observed metrics Hypothesis testing, p-values, confidence intervals
Bayesian Subjective degree of belief / confidence score Explicitly incorporated as a baseline parameter Posterior distributions via updating, credible intervals
Coin Flip Modeling Paradox
  • Frequentist Setup: Flip a coin 1,000 times consecutively. The literal proportion of observed heads forms the unyielding parameter score.
  • Bayesian Setup: Establish a baseline prior belief that a coin is manufactured fair (50% probability). Flip the coin 5 times; use Bayes' Theorem to dynamically transform your prior into an updated posterior probability distribution as fresh event streams roll in.
